M.Sc. - Generative Adversarial Networks for Fine Art Generation

Abstract - Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) have become the de facto tool for image generation. Through the use of deep convolutional networks, such as in the landmark Deep Convolutional GAN (DCGAN) and improvements to GAN training via the Wasserstein metric and other training tips, GANs can achieve impressive results in image super-resolution, restoration and generation. However, these results often do not correlate with quantitative evaluations of GANs, and evaluation of GANs remains an unsolved problem. GANs have recently been applied to the domain of fine art generation, which presents technical, evaluative and even philosophical challenges, as well as an integration with the field of computational creativity. This work provides a thorough quantitative analysis of DCGAN, the state-of-the-art Wasserstein-based GANs, conditional versions thereof, and the GANHacks set of GAN training strategies, through standard quantitative metrics on benchmark data sets. The GANs are trained on the prominent WikiArt data set to determine whether improved GAN performance on traditional data sets translates to improved image quality when trained on art data. The Holistic Evaluation of Art (HEART) is proposed as a method of qualitative evaluation of GAN-generated visual art.

Honours - Using Machine Learning to Predict Performance and Attrition of Front-Line Workers

Abstract - This paper presents the exploration of a front-line worker dataset, and the development of models for predicting worker, primarily community health worker, attrition and performance. Front-line workers are not professionals, but volunteers trained to visit their community to improve health care and developmental services in resource-constrained environments. Dimagi, a for-profit social enterprise, desires to gain insights into attrition and performance problems in this domain. Their dataset is explored and expanded upon using Python and its pandas library. Following this, Stochastic Gradient Descent and LASSO models are trained in the prediction of the values of attrition- and performance-based attributes. It is found that the models are able to predict front-line worker attrition and performance with 87% and 77% accuracy respectively.
